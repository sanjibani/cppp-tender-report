The Big Story

India's procurement has gotten dramatically more competitive

Between 2012 and 2025, the share of government contracts awarded with only a single bidder has fallen from 31.3% to 6.0% — a five-fold improvement. The biggest jump came between 2017 and 2020, coinciding with the introduction of stricter General Financial Rules (GFR) revisions and the rollout of the CPPP 2.0 portal. By 2024, single-bid contracts are below 7.2% across all of central and state procurement combined.

The single-bid metric is a strong corruption signal, but context matters

A "single-bid" award — where only one party submits a proposal — is the single most-cited red flag for pre-selected winners in the procurement corruption literature (Kenny & Musatova, World Bank, 2020). However, it has structural drivers too: defence electronics has qualified-vendor lists; PSUs operating in narrow supply markets routinely see single-source contracts; rate contracts don't compete in the same way as fixed-value tenders.

The 13% national average masks huge departmental variation. Some sectors (Space, Nuclear, Power) run 50-90% single-bid structurally. Defence procurement at scale actually runs only 1.9% single-bid — the narrative of "limited defence competition" does not hold in this data.

Discovery 4 · Captive vendors — the structural single-bid pattern

Six of the top 8 captive vendors are sole-suppliers to one organisation: BHEL EDN

BHEL EDN (Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited, Electronics Division) sources from a closed vendor pool for defence-grade electronics, PCBs, fibre optics, and pharma-grade finishes. The 99.74% single-bid rate at BHEL EDN is explained by this captive supplier ecosystem, not by corruption per se.

But: the value of contracts in this cluster is opaque — many records show Contract Value = 0 (likely rate contracts or framework agreements). The audit question is: what % of these "0-value" contracts actually settle at much higher values through change orders? That's where the structural pattern becomes a transparency concern.

Surprising finding: Counter to the narrative of COVID-era emergency procurement being prone to single-source / no-bid contracts, the data shows April-June 2020 ran ~11% single-bid — lower than the same months in 2019 (12-17%). What this dataset enables that paid databases don't

Cross-portal vendor networks

Same winner appearing across Central + State + Defence portals. Paid databases usually cover only one portal at a time. National-level cartel detection requires this cross-portal view.

Bid window + bid count + corrigendum intersection

The combination of published-to-closing-date gap, number of bids received, and corrigendum frequency on the same tender is the strongest statistical fingerprint. Most public analyses look at these in isolation.

Full award metadata at scale

4.5M records with winner names, addresses, contract values, bid counts — the fields that commercial databases charge subscription fees for are all here.

Time-series at 13-year depth

The longitudinal coverage lets you measure procurement reform impact. The 31%→6% single-bid collapse is not visible in any single-year database snapshot.

What I'd want next

  1. MCA + GSTIN cross-reference for the captive vendors — find common directors, common registered addresses, common PAN. That converts the "structural single-bid" pattern into hard shell-company evidence.
  2. Tender-fee and EMD distributions by sector — actual audit indicators.
  3. Corrigendum timing analysis — corrigenda issued 1-3 days before bid closing are the gold-standard manipulation signal.
  4. Bid-window × contract-value interaction — does the short-window pattern only appear at high values? (Predicted yes; needs quantification.)
  5. State × sector drill-down — Telangana at 94% single-bid warrants much deeper investigation.
